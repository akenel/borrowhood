# From Punch Cards to Electric Dreams

**The story of Angelo Kenel -- 40 years of building things that work.**

---

I started with punch cards.

1983. A kid from a Swiss landscaping family -- son of Albert Kenel, a man who moved earth with a hand shovel and never once complained about the weather. At 16, I had a choice: mud or machines. I chose the machines.

Back then, a program lived on a stack of cardboard rectangles. You didn't "run" code -- you *submitted* it. Then you waited. If you dropped the deck on the way to the reader, God help you. That was debugging in the early 80s: pick up the cards, put them back in order, and try again.

I learned patience from punch cards. I learned precision from my father.

---

## The Long Road

The decades that followed read like a technology timeline:

- **The 80s** -- Punch cards, mainframes, batch jobs. Learning that computers don't forgive sloppy thinking.
- **The 90s** -- PCs, networks, the first taste of the internet. The world got smaller overnight.
- **The 2000s** -- Enterprise systems, databases, the machinery behind the businesses nobody sees but everybody depends on.
- **The 2010s** -- Cloud, containers, the shift from "where does this run" to "does it matter where this runs."
- **The 2020s** -- AI pair programming, infrastructure as code, and the realization that 40 years of muscle memory still matters more than any shortcut.

Every era killed the last one's tools and kept its lessons.

---

## What I Build Now

Today I build **La Piazza** -- a neighbourhood sharing platform where people lend, borrow, teach, and help each other. The way villages used to work, before algorithms decided who your neighbours were.

The stack would make 1983-me fall off his chair:

- **Python & FastAPI** -- the application brain
- **Docker containers** -- ship the whole kitchen, not just the recipe
- **GitHub** -- every change tracked, every decision reversible
- **Keycloak** -- identity and access, because trust has to be engineered
- **Claude CLI** -- an AI co-pilot that writes code at lightning speed while I steer

I went from feeding punch cards into a reader to pair-programming with an AI that lives in my terminal. The gap between those two realities is 40 years, mass, and I carry every one of them.

---

## The Philosophy

My father built retaining walls by hand. Stone by stone, level by level. No shortcuts. If the foundation was wrong, you ripped it out and started over -- you didn't stack more stones on top and hope.

Software is the same. I've watched teams duct-tape systems together for decades. Ship it, patch it, pray. That's not engineering. That's gambling.

**I build things that work.** Not things that demo well. Not things that pass the first test and fall apart under load. Things that *work* -- in production, at midnight, when nobody's watching.

---

## Why La Piazza

Because technology should bring people closer, not further apart.

Because a kid who needs a drill for one afternoon shouldn't have to buy one. Because a retired welder with 30 years of skill shouldn't sit idle while someone struggles with a broken bike frame. Because the best marketplace is the one where your neighbour already has what you need.

La Piazza is the town square, digitized. No algorithms deciding what you see. No engagement metrics optimizing for addiction. Just people, in a neighbourhood, helping each other out.

---

## The Punch Line

Forty years ago, I fed instructions to a machine one card at a time and waited for it to tell me I got it wrong.

Today, I describe what I want to build, and an AI writes the first draft in seconds. I still have to tell it when it gets it wrong -- some things never change.

But the dream is the same dream it always was: **build something useful, build it right, and make sure it still works tomorrow.**

From punch cards to electric dreams. The tools changed. The standard didn't.
