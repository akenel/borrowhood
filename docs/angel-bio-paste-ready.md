I started with punch cards in 1983. A kid from a Swiss landscaping family -- son of Albert Kenel, a man who moved earth with a hand shovel. At 16, I had a choice: mud or machines. I chose the machines.

**The Long Road**

- **The 80s** -- Punch cards, mainframes, batch jobs
- **The 90s** -- PCs, networks, the first taste of the internet
- **The 2000s** -- Enterprise systems, databases, the machinery behind businesses
- **The 2010s** -- Cloud, containers, infrastructure as code
- **The 2020s** -- AI pair programming with Claude CLI at lightning speed

Every era killed the last one's tools and kept its lessons.

**What I Build Now**

Today I build **La Piazza** -- a neighbourhood sharing platform where people lend, borrow, teach, and help each other. The way villages used to work, before algorithms decided who your neighbours were.

The stack: **Python & FastAPI**, **Docker**, **GitHub**, **Keycloak**, and **Claude CLI** as my AI co-pilot. I went from feeding punch cards into a reader to pair-programming with an AI that lives in my terminal.

**The Philosophy**

My father built retaining walls by hand. Stone by stone, level by level. No shortcuts. If the foundation was wrong, you ripped it out and started over.

Software is the same. **I build things that work.** Not things that demo well. Things that work -- in production, at midnight, when nobody's watching.

**Why La Piazza**

Because a kid who needs a drill for one afternoon shouldn't have to buy one. Because a retired welder with 30 years of skill shouldn't sit idle while someone struggles with a broken bike frame.

La Piazza is the town square, digitized. Just people, in a neighbourhood, helping each other out.

**From punch cards to electric dreams. The tools changed. The standard didn't.**
